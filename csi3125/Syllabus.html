CSI 3125. CONCEPTS OF PROGRAMMING LANGUAGES
(3 hours of lectures per week, 2 hours of labs per week, 4 credits).

Classification of programming languages. Syntactic description of programming languages. Imperative programming languages: data objects, data types, control structures, sub-programs, principles of implementation. Logic programming languages. Functional programming languages. Object-oriented programming languages. Modules. Prerequisites: CSI 2114, two programming language labs selected among CSI 2165, (CSI2171 or CSI2172 or CSI2173) and CSI2174. Cannot be combined for credits with CSI 2115.

Course objectives

Present the fundamental issues in the design and use of major programming languages. Introduce several important programming languages with a difference. Prepare you for further study of programming languages, help develop an appreciation of a programming language as a tool for software construction, enable you to evaluate and choose a language to match the problem.

Marking scheme

A maximum of 100 marks will be available. The division is as follows:

[HW] homework 40 marks
[MD] midterm exam (closed book, 90 min.) 20 marks
[FN] final exam (closed book, 3 hours) 40 marks

The midterm will cover topics 1-8. The final exam will be comprehensive, with a slight emphasis on topics 9-15. Topics are listed later in this document.

Homework is also listed in more detail later in this document. All homework will be posted at the course Web site, usually a week in advance. Each homework is worth 10 marks.

The policy of the School of Information Technology and Engineering requires you to pass the controlled evaluation component, that is, to achieve at least 50% of the available test and exam marks. The grade will be calculated according to the usual University of Ottawa scale (A+ for 90% and above, failure with a D or lower for less than 55%). The basis for the grade will be the adjusted total AT, computed as follows:

if MD + FN < 30
then AT = (MD + FN ) * 1.6
else AT = MD + FN + HW;

Topics and reading

You are expected to read the indicated chapters or their sections right after the material has been discussed -- or assigned for home reading -- in class. Feel free to be slightly ahead of lectures (:->). There will be richer class notes for a few topics that are not covered well in the textbook.

    Review of background knowledge

  2. Preliminaries

  4. Describing the syntax of programming languages
    (sections 3.1-3.4).

  5. Describing the semantics of programming languages
    (section 3.6.2).

  6. An introduction to Scheme
    (sections 14.1-14.6).

  7. An introduction to ML

    (chapter 4).

    (chapter 5).

    (chapter 7).

  13. Subprograms
    (chapter 8).

  14. Implementing subprograms
    (sections 9.3-9.4).

    (fragments of chapter 10).
