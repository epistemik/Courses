 Course Goal

This course will introduce you to the main ideas in KE (Knowledge Engineering) and KM (Knowledge Management), both practical and theoretical. After this course you should be much better prepared to deal with any situation involving putting structured knowledge into computers so they can be active participants in managing it.

 Readings

I may make some readings available later that are not on line.

 Text

Knowledge Representation: Logical, Philosophical, and Computational Foundations. John Sowa. Brookes/Cole, Pacific Grove, 2000. This is a very complex and deep discussion of both the underlying theory of knowledge and practical aspects of knowledge engineering. The author has worked in this field for thirty years, and is in many ways the leading authority. There is no other book like it.

Every student will make a presentation on their project (about 20  min) in the last two weeks of class.  (25 marks for project; 5 marks for presentation)

 SOME BIG QUESTIONS (We will answer)

 

  • What is knowledge? How do we encode it?

  • How can computers help us with it?

  • How is KM (K Management) typically done today? 

  • What theory is available? What disciplines contribute? 

  • What are knowledge bases?  How are they different from  databases? 

  • How can we share k? Agree on it? Standardize it?

  • How can we know if a kb is correct or complete?

  • What are expert systems?

  • What is a knowledge engineer?

  • What is an ontology?

  • What’s the difference between KE and KM?

  • What tools are available for KE and KM?

  • What areas of CS can benefit from KM?

  • What other areas can benefit? (Example: engineering)
