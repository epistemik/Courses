All logics are concerned with proving, i.e a sequence of valid inference steps (one application of an inf rule) leading to a desired conclusion.

Predicate calculus  (calcul des prédicats)  (first-order) (premier ordre  m)(pc or fol) is the lingua franca of k rep. You should always know how any k rep can be expressed in fol. 

propositional logic (logique des propositions) is a simple logic with no variables. It may be thought of as a subset of pc

 predicate: (prédicat   m)        an abstraction of a natural language statement. Predicates say something about their arguments. Usually comes from a noun, a verb, or an adjective.

 

            man                   man(X)                 X is a man

            walks               walks(X)               X walks

            big                     big(X)                    X is big

            father of             father-of(X,Y)        X is father of Y

            knows               knows(X,Y)           X knows Y

Variables and quantification

 

Pred calc improves on prop calc by introducing arguments, called terms.

A term names something,  either

 A constant

       1, a, ottawa, tom

 A variable

       X, y, var1

A functional term

       F(x), 2*3+1, father of john and mary

 

Variables stand for something unspecified. They have a scope in which they mean the same thing. Often the scope is not explicit: you must know the conventions.

 Quantification: vars are either:

           universally quantified (any substitution allowed within scope)

          "x"y  likes(x,y)          anything x likes anything y

from this we can conclude, e.g. 

          likes(saddam,bush)

          likes(my vacuum cleaner, 2)

 

          existentially quantified (something particular is being refered

          to, usually depending on something else). Acts like a constant.

           $x interesting(x) & difficult(x)

                    there exists something that is interesting and difficult

 the only thing we can conclude from this is

           $x interesting(x)

          $x difficult(x)

           "x $y  likes(x,y)          anything x likes something y (that

                    depends on x)

here, the y depends on the x, i.e. choose any x, then there is a corresponding y (which may or may not belong to other choices for x) 

rule: any $ var following a " var depends on it

free (not quantified and hence uncommitted)

all they say is that the same thing is being refered to in more than one place

 

Rules of Inference

rule of inference               (régle d’inférences  f) a rule that permits concluding (conclure) a new statement based on the truth of one or more others (an inf step) (pas d'inférence).

 Ex:             modus ponens            

P => Q

                                 P

_______

                                         Q

resolution  (résolution f) is a generalization of mp (P => Q is defined as -P V Q):

                                                 -P V Q

                                                P

                                                ______

                                                Q

or similarly with -Q giving  -P

 clause            (clause  f) a formula with no ands and no quantifiers (quantificateur  m); only ors and nots.

            clause 1                  A1, A2, A3, ....An

            clause 2                  A1, A2, -A3, .....Am

                                          ________________

            resolvent               A1, A2, ...An, ...Am (all except A3)

 

The elements of clauses are called literals. (littéral )

 

Resolution in PC

Can do forward or backward resolution, however usually do backward:

assuming the axioms are in clausal form (forme clausale  f), we try to obtain a contradiction (contradiction f) of the negated (nié) goal with the axioms. If we do and the axioms are consistent (logique), then the goal must be true.

Example

                        P(X)                    X is prime

                        O(X)                    X is odd

                        X = Y                     equality

                        s(X)                       the square function (normally written X2)

 any prime except 2 is odd:

            All X             P(X) & ~ X=2 => O(X)

 

if a number is odd, its square is odd:

             All X                     O(X) => O(s(X))    note X assumed to be a number

 7 is a prime (not odd):

             P(7)

 ~ 7 = 2             (logic needs everything stated!!!!)

 Prove:  the square of 7 is odd.

 In clause form:

 1          ~ P(X) v X = 2 v O(X)

 2            ~O(X) v O(s(X))

 3            P(7)

 4          ~ 7 = 2

 neg goal (5)  ~O(s(7))

 Proof (preuve f):

            1 R 3 yields 7 = 2 v O(7)                 x/7

            6 R 4                      O(7)

            O(7) R 2              O(s(7))                        x/7

            O(s(7)) R 5           nil
